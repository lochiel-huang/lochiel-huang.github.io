CASE 01

AI-powered Financial Compliance Auditor

Role: AI Product Manager · Personal prototype · In development (2026)

The problem. Even in mid-sized financial and property-management firms, compliance review still runs on paper. Vouchers, purchase requisitions, invoices, bank slips, and approval forms are physically stapled together, cross-checked by eye, and annotated with sticky notes. Traditional OCR fails on stamped or handwritten regions; account codes are inconsistent across sources; split reimbursements (e.g. two employees' travel expenses paid asymmetrically but summing to the correct total) break rule-based reconciliation engines entirely.

Design

A three-stage agent workflow: (1) multi-modal parsing extracts fields and stamps them with confidence scores; (2) a fuzzy-matching layer combines semantic embedding search (for account-code aliases) with a deterministic Python solver (for split-and-merge arithmetic); (3) a compliance-review agent generates virtual sticky notes — machine-authored annotations that mirror what a human auditor would handwrite, waiting for a one-click confirmation instead of silent auto-approval.

Why this shape, not another
  • Anti-hallucination on numbers. LLMs do arithmetic. The prototype does not let them. All numeric matching runs through a deterministic solver; the LLM only handles language and layout.
  • Human-in-the-loop by default. Confidence below threshold triggers a highlighted review, never a silent pass. Compliance is a domain where "quiet failures" are the failures.
  • Cold-start realistic. A separate data-cleaning agent normalises the messy Excel history that almost every early-stage firm actually has, before anything else runs.
Where it came from

The concept crystallised during my compliance internship at Huafu International Financial Holdings and my accounting internship at Sheraton Shanghai Jiading. I kept noticing the same shape of problem across contexts: the auditor's real work isn't judgement — it's the mechanical assembly of the evidence needed for judgement.

Stack: Python · OCR · RAG · LLM orchestration STATUS: Prototype in progress
CASE 02

Field Observation Report: Social Interaction Patterns

Role: Observer & Analyst · Personal research · Documented (June 2026)

The setting. A late-night social venue. A stranger initiated contact and, over the following two hours, deployed a sequence of persuasion tactics: a purity narrative about a chaste prior relationship, appeals to feminist principles, criticism of materialistic marriage customs, and a hypothetical "if you were my wife" framing — before making a physically escalating request shortly before departure. My interest was not in the individual encounter but in the structure: how consistently the tactics escalated, how the earlier statements retroactively looked like scaffolding for the later ones.

Method

Following the encounter, I wrote up the sequence as a structured behavioural log: numbered event codes, timestamps, verbal and non-verbal moves, and a distinction between observed behaviour and observer inference. I coded the pattern using a small ad-hoc taxonomy adapted from the courtship-behaviour literature I'd read during my psychology self-study, and included a confidentiality note about the limits of a single-case observation.

Why this counts as work
  • Discomfort as data. The impulse to react emotionally is real; the value is in converting it into something structured that someone else could read, disagree with, and interrogate.
  • Method transfer. The same taxonomy-and-annotation reflex is what I use in compliance work. The medium is different; the discipline is identical.
  • Honest scope. The report explicitly states its evidentiary limits — one case is not a pattern; a suspicious sequence is not a proof of intent. That kind of hedging is what makes a case study useful rather than performative.
Format: Structured behavioural log · Anonymised STATUS: Documented
